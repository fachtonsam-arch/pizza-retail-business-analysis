The business generated total annual revenue across all pizza categories and sizes throughout the 2015 fiscal year. Revenue is driven by a combination of order volume and average unit price, with the Classic category contributing the largest share due to high order frequency.

1b. Total Orders & Quantity Sold

?
Research Question
How many orders were placed and how many individual pizza units were sold in 2015? What does this reveal about the scale and volume of the business?
▸ Dashboard: Sales Trends — KPI Cards

The dataset captures all orders placed during 2015, with each order potentially containing multiple pizza items. Total quantity sold reflects the sum of all individual pizza units across every order. These headline figures establish the operational scale of the business and serve as the baseline for all subsequent analysis.

1c. Average Order Value

?
Research Question
What is the average spend per order? How does this metric inform pricing strategy and identify opportunities for upselling?
▸ Dashboard: Sales Trends — KPI Cards

The average order value (AOV) is calculated as total revenue divided by the number of distinct orders. AOV is a key commercial metric — it reflects the typical spend per customer visit and can be improved through upselling, bundle promotions, and premium product placement. The AOV for this business is consistent with a casual dining / delivery pizza model.

Context
These top-level KPIs provide the executive summary of business performance. All section analyses in this report are grounded in these baseline figures, enabling proportional comparisons across categories, time periods, and order types.

Section 2 — Sales Trends Analysis

2b. Peak Sales Periods

?
Research Question
When during the year does daily revenue peak? Are there seasonal patterns that could be leveraged to optimise promotional planning and inventory management?
▸ Dashboard: Sales Trends — Sales Trends across 2015

The peak sales period occurred on 27 November (Thanksgiving weekend), recording the single highest daily revenue of the year. A secondary seasonal peak is visible in July, coinciding with summer demand. Revenue dips notably in September–October, suggesting a mid-year slowdown before the holiday uplift.

Key Insight
Promotional activity timed around Thanksgiving and summer months would likely yield the highest return on marketing spend.

2c. Hourly Sales Pattern

?
Research Question
At what times of day are customers most likely to place orders? How can kitchen staffing and capacity be structured around intraday demand peaks?
▸ Dashboard: Sales Trends — Sales Trend per Hour

Order volume follows a clear intraday pattern. Activity begins around 11:00 (lunch), peaks sharply at 12:00–13:00, dips in the mid-afternoon, then rises again at 17:00–19:00 (dinner rush). Volume drops significantly after 21:00. The lunch window (11:00–14:00) and dinner window (17:00–21:00) together account for the majority of daily orders.

2d. Weekday vs Weekend Trends

?
Research Question
Which days of the week generate the most revenue? Should staffing levels and inventory orders be adjusted to match weekly demand variation?
▸ Dashboard: Sales Trends — Sales Trend by Weekday

Friday consistently generates the highest revenue across all weeks, followed by Thursday and Saturday. Monday and Tuesday show the lowest order volumes, suggesting staffing and inventory can be optimised for lighter weekday demand.

Section 4 — Ingredient & Size Analysis

4a. Size Distribution

?
Research Question
Which pizza sizes are most popular within each category? Do customers prefer larger or smaller formats depending on pizza style, and how should promotional bundles be designed accordingly?
▸ Dashboard: Pizza Ranking — Sales Volume of different Pizza categories

Smaller sizes (S, M, L) dominate sales volume across most categories. Classic and Veggie pizzas are most frequently ordered in Large (L), while Supreme and Chicken categories see stronger Medium (M) demand. XL and XXL sizes contribute the least volume overall but command a higher price per unit, making them margin-positive for the business.

Recommendation
Focus promotional bundles on M and L sizes for Classic and Veggie — these sizes drive volume. Reserve XL/XXL marketing for premium customer segments.

Section 5 — Price–Volume Correlation

5a. Top & Bottom Ranking Groups

?

▸ Dashboard: Ingredient Analysis — Ingredients Analysis

Top-5 pizzas by revenue and quantity include Classic Deluxe, Barbecue Chicken, Thai Chicken, California Chicken, and Spicy Italian. 5b. Scatter Analysis: Avg Price vs Total Quantity by Category

?
Research Question
Is there a measurable relationship between average pizza price and total sales volume across categories? Does price elasticity drive purchasing decisions, and if so, how strong is that effect?
▸ Dashboard: Sales Efficiency — Correlation of Pizzas Value & Volume / Correlation Coefficient (r)

The scatter chart reveals a negative linear relationship between average pizza price and total sales volume across the four categories. Categories with lower average prices — notably Classic — achieve the highest sales volumes, while premium-priced categories such as Chicken and Supreme show lower volumes despite higher per-unit revenue.

The Correlation Coefficient (r) quantifies this relationship. A value close to −1 indicates a strong inverse relationship between price and demand, consistent with standard price elasticity behaviour.

Implication
Price sensitivity is a significant driver of purchasing behaviour. Modest price reductions on Chicken and Supreme categories could drive volume uplift that offsets the reduced margin per unit. Classic pizzas, already high-volume, are best positioned for incremental price optimisation.

Section 6 — Order Behaviour Analysis

6a. Single-Item vs Multi-Item Orders

?
Research Question
What proportion of orders contain only one unique pizza type versus multiple varieties? Does this reveal a customer tendency toward variety purchasing that could be exploited through bundle promotions?
▸ Dashboard: Sales Efficiency — Order Size Segmentation

Order composition analysis distinguishes between:

  • Single-Item orders — exactly one unique pizza type per order
  • Multi-Item orders — two or more distinct pizza types in the same order

Multi-item orders represent the majority of transactions, indicating that most customers purchase multiple distinct pizza varieties per visit. This pattern supports upselling strategies — for instance, "Add a second pizza" prompts at checkout — as customers are already predisposed to variety purchasing.

Recommendation
Design bundle promotions targeting multi-item buyers. A "Meal Deal" pairing a Classic with a Chicken or Supreme pizza could increase average order value while reinforcing existing purchase behaviour.

6b. Average Time Between Orders

?
Research Question
How quickly are consecutive orders placed within the same day? Does order frequency slow during peak hours — indicating a kitchen bottleneck — and how can this inform staffing and equipment decisions?
▸ Dashboard: Sales Efficiency — Avg Time Between Orders

Calculated only within the same day (excluding overnight gaps between the last order of one day and the first of the next), the average time between consecutive orders reflects genuine operational throughput. This metric is a proxy for kitchen capacity utilisation — shorter intervals indicate higher sustained throughput; longer intervals may flag bottlenecks during peak hours.

Comparing this value against the peak-hour (12:00–13:00 and 18:00–20:00) same-day intervals would identify whether throughput slows during peak demand, helping to size staffing and equipment requirements.
